European Cities Temperature Analysis with PCA

Climate classification of European cities using Principal Component Analysis

View Code on GitHub Download PDF Report

Project Summary

This project applies Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to monthly temperature data from European cities to identify climate patterns based on geographical location. The analysis reveals how European cities cluster according to temperature characteristics and geographical factors, reducing 16 dimensions to just 2 principal components that capture 93.9% of the variance.

Part 1: Exploratory Analysis & Correlations

Challenge

Understand the relationships between the 16 variables in the dataset (12 months + average + amplitude + latitude + longitude) before applying PCA.

Correlation Analysis

Initial analysis revealed important patterns:

  • High correlation between months: Months show correlations >0.95 with each other, especially adjacent months
  • Latitude as dominant factor: Strong negative correlation with all temperature variables
  • Thermal amplitude: Low correlation with most variables except specific months
  • Longitude: Minimal influence on annual average temperatures

Preprocessing

To prepare data for PCA:

  • Removal of irrelevant "Region" column
  • Standardization of all variables (centering and scaling)
  • Separation of city names for subsequent analysis

Initial Insights

Seasonal Patterns

High correlations between months confirm consistent seasonal patterns. Winter months (January, February) show the lowest correlations with summer months (June, July), as expected.

Geographical Determinants

Latitude emerges as the most influential factor, while longitude has minimal impact. This confirms that north-south position is much more determinant for climate than east-west position in Europe.

Part 2: PCA Application & Component Selection

Challenge

Determine the optimal number of principal components to retain for analysis, balancing explained variance and simplification.

Selection Criteria

Three criteria were applied to determine the number of components:

  • Kaiser Criterion: Retain components with eigenvalues > 1 (2 components)
  • Elbow Method: Graphical analysis of eigenvalue curve (3 components)
  • Cumulative Variance: Seek >90% explained variance (2 components: 93.9%)

Final Decision

2 principal components were selected because:

  • They explain 93.9% of total variance
  • They comply with Kaiser criterion (eigenvalues > 1)
  • They provide optimal simplification while maintaining essential information

Component Results

PC1 Interpretation

The first component (78.0% variance) represents the north-south temperature gradient. It is strongly correlated with annual average temperature and all months, and shows a strong negative correlation with latitude.

PC2 Interpretation

The second component (15.9% variance) represents continentality vs. maritime influence. It is related to annual thermal amplitude and shows distinct patterns between continental and coastal cities.

Part 3: Interpretation & City Classification

Challenge

Interpret the principal components and classify European cities according to their climate characteristics.

Observation Projection

Cities were projected onto the plane formed by the first two principal components:

  • Positive PC1 extreme: Southern cities (Seville, Athens, Palermo) - warmer
  • Negative PC1 extreme: Northern cities (Reykjavik, St. Petersburg, Helsinki) - colder
  • Positive PC2 extreme: Continental cities (Kiev, Budapest) - higher thermal amplitude
  • Negative PC2 extreme: Maritime cities (Dublin, Reykjavik, Edinburgh) - lower amplitude

Climate Classification

Cities naturally clustered into four quadrants representing combinations of the two components:

Quadrant Climate Type Representative Examples Characteristics
Q1 (PC1+, PC2+) Warm + Continental Athens, Budapest, Rome, Madrid High temperatures, large thermal amplitude
Q2 (PC1-, PC2+) Cold + Continental Helsinki, Kiev, Moscow, Stockholm Low temperatures, large thermal amplitude
Q3 (PC1-, PC2-) Cold + Maritime Amsterdam, Berlin, Dublin, London Low temperatures, low thermal amplitude
Q4 (PC1+, PC2-) Warm + Maritime Lisbon, Paris, Barcelona, Bordeaux High temperatures, low thermal amplitude

Correlation Circle

Correlation circle analysis confirmed:

  • Well-represented variables: Average temperature, individual months, latitude
  • Relationships between variables: Natural grouping of months by seasons
  • Contributions to components: Identification of which variables define each axis

Quality of Representation (COS²)

All cities showed COS² values > 0.9 on at least one axis, indicating excellent representation in the reduced space. Cities like Oslo, Stockholm, and Genoa achieved perfect values of 1.0 on one of the components.

Interesting Exceptions

Budapest and Milan appeared as "Continental South" despite not being the most southern cities. Geneva was classified as "Continental North" due to its altitude and distance from the sea, confirming that altitude can modulate the effect of latitude.

General Conclusions

Dual Climate Classification

European cities can be classified simultaneously using two main axes: north-south (average temperature) and continental-maritime (thermal amplitude). This two-dimensional classification captures the essence of European climate patterns.

Clear Geographical Patterns

Four main climate groups were identified with coherent geographical distributions: Northwest (cold+maritime), Northeast (cold+continental), Southwest (warm+maritime), and Southeast (warm+continental).

Confirmation of Climate Theories

The analysis confirms established climate theories: oceanic influence softens temperatures (low amplitude), continentality extremizes temperatures (high amplitude), and latitude is the dominant factor in average temperatures.

Value of PCA Analysis

PCA proved to be an extremely effective tool for reducing the complexity of multidimensional climate data, revealing underlying patterns that are not immediately apparent in univariate analysis. The ability to visualize complex relationships in a two-dimensional space facilitates interpretation and communication of results.

Practical Applications

This methodology can be extended to other geographical and climate datasets, allowing comparisons between regions, identification of climate anomalies, and support for decision-making in urban planning, tourism, and climate change studies.
